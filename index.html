Marine timeline

The week's weather, on one map.

Small Craft Advisories aren't a wall of text in RipCat — they're a timeline you can scrub. Drag through the forecast and watch advisories sweep across the coast, zone by zone, from this morning to the weekend.

  • Zones painted on the chart — advisory areas glow where and when they're in effect.
  • Press play and let the week run like a weather-desk loop.
  • Buoy chips live on the same map — water temp and seas next to every zone.

Buoy detail

Every buoy is a weather station. Now it reports to you.

Tap buoy 46053 off Santa Barbara and get the full briefing: swell height, direction, and period on a compass rose, wind and gusts, pressure and its trend, water, air, and dew point — plus the last twelve hours of wave history to see where it's headed.

  • Water temperature with a 7-day trend — know if it's been building before you drive.
  • Primary swell vs wind waves, separated, with period bars.
  • Rearrange the sections so your number is always on top.

At a glance

The whole forecast, before your coffee cools.

Tap a zone and the oracle lays it out: an outlook-by-day strip that flags every advisory window at a glance, then the full daily forecast — winds, seas, and wave detail in the National Weather Service's own words.

  • Outlook by day — morning, afternoon, evening chips for the whole week.
  • Severity you can see — coded by shape and color, labeled for VoiceOver.
  • Cached offline — the last briefing stays with you past the breakwater.

Make it yours

Your units, your theme, your month on one page.

Feet or meters, 12 or 24-hour time, four chart themes from light to full nautical. And when you'd rather trust paper, print a tide calendar — a whole month of curves, highs, lows, and sun times, ink-friendly, for the fridge, the nav desk, or the tackle box.

  • Chart themes: Light, Dark, Coastal, Nautical.
  • High-tide alerts and a refresh cadence you control.
  • Tide calendars with sunrise, sunset, and every turn of the water.

The story

Made by ocean people. And one very unimpressed cat.

RipCat is built in Santa Barbara by Ben Williamson — a surfer and developer — with his daughter Odette and their cat Rip, a tabby who has seen bigger waves and is not impressed. (Kitten apprentice BlueJazz remains convinced the ocean is a giant water bowl.)

Rip's board is shaped by Randy Cone, who has been shaping Ben's boards for decades. The hoodie is from Santa Barbara Surf Shop — surfboards by Yater since 1962. Real shop, real shaper, real cat.

"Know the tide. Read the water. Go at the right time."

No venture money, no growth team, no dark patterns. Just an app the three of them actually use every morning — and keep making better.
