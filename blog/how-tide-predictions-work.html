Tide basics

How tide predictions work — and why they are not guarantees.

Tide tables feel absolute because they print exact times and heights. The ocean is less tidy than that. A prediction tells you what the astronomical tide should do at a station; the real water can still be nudged by wind, pressure, swell, runoff, and local geography.

Published July 7, 2026 · NOAA/NOS sources · Not for navigation

Not for navigation: this is plain-English planning context, not a substitute for official charts, local notices, seamanship, or current conditions. Tide predictions are predictions, not guarantees.

The short version: NOAA tide predictions are built from measurements at tide stations and the repeating pull of the moon and sun. Once a station has enough observed water-level history, NOAA can model the main tidal constituents — the regular ingredients that combine into the highs, lows, and in-between curve you see every day.

That is why tomorrow's low at Santa Barbara can be predicted down to the minute, and why the next low will not be exactly six hours later. The moon is the big drummer, the sun adds its own rhythm, and the shape of the coast turns that rhythm into something local. Harbors, points, channels, and bays all bend the same ocean into different timing.

Predicted tide height is station-specific.

A tide prediction belongs to a station, not a whole coastline. If you are reading a prediction for a harbor station, it is telling you the expected water level at that station's reference point. Around the corner, inside a slough, or across a shallow bar, the timing and useful depth can be different.

That is why RipCat is built around favorites and a map instead of one generic beach tide. The spot matters. A surfer watching a point, an angler walking a sand flat, and a sailor timing a channel are all reading the same kind of curve, but each one cares about a different part of it.

The curve matters more than the stamp.

People often ask, "What time is low tide?" Good question — incomplete answer. The better question is: how fast is the water moving around that low?

A flat slack around low can feel dead for fishing and shallow for a harbor transit. The same printed low followed by a strong push can be the whole reason to go. For surf, a wave that hates the bottom of the tide may turn on halfway through the fill. The exact high/low stamps are useful, but the slope of the curve is what tells you the window.

Weather can move real water away from the prediction.

The astronomical tide is only one force on the water. Wind can pile water against a coast or push it out of a bay. Low pressure can let water stand higher than expected; high pressure can do the opposite. Big swell and long-period energy can change what a beach, inlet, or harbor entrance feels like even if the predicted tide is normal.

That does not make tide predictions bad. It makes them one layer. For anything on the water, especially around small craft conditions, you want the prediction next to the official marine forecast and the live buoys. A tide app should not pretend a pretty curve is the whole ocean.

Rip's rule: read the tide, then read the water.

Use the prediction to pick the likely window. Then check the buoy, the wind, the advisory, and what your eyes are telling you at the spot. Rip has seen bigger waves and is not impressed by false confidence.

Why two nearby places can feel different.

The tide wave moves along the coast, and the coast is not a straight bathtub. Points, islands, harbor mouths, shallow shelves, and long bays all affect how that water shows up. A tide station at the pier may be the best official reference nearby, but your launch ramp or reef can still have local quirks.

Good local knowledge still wins. If your spot needs three feet and rising, write that into your own mental playbook. RipCat helps by making the station curve easy to check every time, across your phone, watch, widgets, and Mac, so your local read has a clean baseline.

How to use a tide prediction well.

  • Start with the right station. Pick the closest useful NOAA station, not just the nearest pin by mileage.
  • Look at the slope. Fast rise or fall can matter more than the exact high/low time.
  • Check the weather layer. Wind, pressure, swell, and NWS marine advisories can change the day.
  • Respect margins. If a shallow channel, bar, or rocky walk needs precision, do not cut it close.
  • Use official sources for decisions that can hurt people or boats. Predictions help planning; they do not replace judgment.

The tide is predictable enough to plan your morning around. It is not predictable enough to outsource responsibility. That is the honest middle: know the window, check the rest, and go at the right time.
